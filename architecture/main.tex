% === ABSTRACT ===
\begin{abstract}
\noindent
In every computer ever built, four subsystems are designed
\emph{independently} and then forced to coexist: the interconnect topology,
the error-correcting code, the instruction set, and --- for learning
machines --- the adaptation rule. Each is separately optimised; their
mutual mismatch is a permanent tax paid in silicon, energy, and
verification effort. This document reports a single structural fact,
forced by the theorems of Unitary Holonomic Monism (UHM), that erases the
tax: on a seven-mode substrate these four are not four things but
\emph{one} --- the Fano plane $\Fano$ is simultaneously the interconnect
(its seven lines), the error-correcting code (Hamming $[7,4,3]$, of which
it is the parity-check geometry), the operator algebra of the instruction
(the $G_2$-covariant Lindbladian $\LindOmega$ on $\Gam\in\Dens(\mathbb{C}^7)$),
and, through the fixed-point-seeking regeneration term, the learning rule.
We call this collapse \emph{architectural monism} and prove it is
\emph{derived}, not designed (\S\ref{sec:collapse}).

The resulting architecture, \TALOS{}, is post--von-Neumann by construction:
the state \emph{is} the memory and its evolution \emph{is} the computation,
so there is no fetch--execute cycle and no von~Neumann bottleneck; the
state of one holon is a $7\times7$ Hermitian density matrix
(\DM{}, $784$~bytes, $34$ physical parameters) that fits in L1 cache. The
single instruction $\LindOmega = -i[H_{\mathrm{eff}},\Gam] + \Diss[\Gam] +
\Regen[\Gam]$ decomposes into a Landauer-reversible unitary core and two
entropy-exporting terms (forgetting and learning); consequently
\textbf{energy scales with learning, not with compute} --- a substrate at
its fixed point computes for free at the Landauer floor
($kT\ln 2 \approx 2.9\times10^{-21}$~J/bit), some nine orders of magnitude
below a single off-chip memory access on a von~Neumann machine
(\S\ref{sec:energy}). Fault tolerance to distance three costs \emph{zero}
extra hardware because the wiring is the code (\S\ref{sec:ecc}); integration
per operation is $9\times$ that of a line-blind router (\S\ref{sec:fabric}).

We give the honest boundaries up front. UHM's substrate-closure theorem
(T-153) proves the computation is substrate-\emph{independent}: there is no
unique physical machine, only a unique \emph{logical} architecture and a
family of substrates that realise it; \TALOS{} is the impedance-matched
member of that family, not the only one. UHM's computational bound proves
the regeneration term grants \emph{no} speed-up beyond the class BQP: the
advantage is energy, adaptivity, and fault-tolerance, never complexity
class. \TALOS{} is the hardware body of the \SYNARC{} mind; the two are
co-designed and the application binary interface between them is specified
in full (\S\ref{sec:abi}). Every quantitative claim is machine-checked; the
design survives an explicit red-team (\S\ref{sec:redteam}) and reduces to a
handful of falsifiable engineering benchmarks against a von~Neumann
baseline (\S\ref{sec:benchmarks}).
\end{abstract}

\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=gray!5, colframe=gray!50, title={\textbf{For the engineer, in one paragraph}}]
Think of a chip whose entire architectural state is one $784$-byte matrix
living in registers, evolving by one differential-equation ``tick'' that is
at once its ALU, its network, its ECC, and its optimiser. The network is
seven shared buses (not an all-to-all crossbar); those same seven buses
\emph{are} a Hamming code, so any single fault is located by three parity
checks with no added storage; the tick's reversible half burns no Landauer
energy, so the machine's power draw tracks how fast it is \emph{learning},
not how hard it is \emph{computing}; and learning is not a separate
training loop but the third term of the one instruction. You do not
\emph{choose} this arrangement --- given a seven-fold self-modelling state,
the mathematics forces it. That is what this document specifies, and how it
plugs into \SYNARC{}.
\end{tcolorbox}
